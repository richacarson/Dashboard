
# Bull Market Position & Bear Market Risk Assessment

## Thesis

The S&P 500 crossed 7,000 for the first time on April 15, 2026, closing at 7,064 on April 21. The current bull market from the October 2022 low has delivered ~98% returns over ~3.5 years — further than the median bull (78%) but well short of the average (121%) or the biggest runs (495%). The question isn't whether a bear market will come, but what kind: a **normal bear** (~27% drop, recovers in 1-2 years) or a **crisis bear** (~51% drop, 5-7 years to recover). The current environment has elements of both — private credit stress is real (defaults at 5.8%, highest on record) but the financial plumbing hasn't broken yet. The Iran War and elevated oil prices add a wild card the Fed can't easily fix.

**Key insight:** Bull market length is a weak predictor of bear market depth. What matters is **what causes the bear** — specifically, whether it's something the Fed can fix quickly (normal bear) or not (crisis bear).

## Risks to This Analysis

1. **Bull market extends.** CAPE of 37 is expensive but was 44 at the dot-com peak. AI capex could drive earnings growth that justifies valuations. If earnings grow 15%+ in 2026-27, the bull has room to run.

2. **Ceasefire rally.** If Hormuz reopens, oil drops to $70, and inflation moderates, the Fed cuts, and risk assets rip. Being too defensive costs performance.

3. **Private credit is contained.** The $3T private credit market may bend but not break. Institutional investors (pensions, endowments) have longer time horizons than retail depositors. Unlike 2008, there's no systemic leverage through the banking system.

4. **AI changes the cycle.** If AI productivity gains are real and material, corporate earnings could grow through a mild recession, shortening or preventing a bear market entirely.
