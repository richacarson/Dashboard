
# Qualified Opportunity Funds — The Roth IRA's Bigger, Bolder Cousin

## Thesis

Qualified Opportunity Funds (QOFs) are the single most powerful tax-advantaged vehicle available to high-net-worth investors with realized capital gains. Unlike a Roth IRA — which caps contributions at $7,000/year ($8,000 if 50+) and requires earned income — a QOF has **no contribution limit**, accepts **any capital gain** (short-term, long-term, Section 1231, carried interest), and after a 10-year hold, **all appreciation is permanently tax-free**. With OZ 2.0 signed into law on July 4, 2025 via the "One Big Beautiful Bill Act," the program has been permanently extended with new incentives. For IOWN's client base — faith-driven business owners building 100+ year family legacies — this is a generational wealth-building tool that deserves dedicated attention.

## How Qualified Opportunity Funds Work

### The Basic Mechanics

1. **Trigger event:** Investor realizes a capital gain (stock sale, business sale, real estate, crypto — any recognized gain)
2. **180-day window:** Investor has 180 days from the date of the gain to invest the gain amount into a QOF
3. **Deferral:** The original capital gain is deferred — not owed until the earlier of: (a) the date the QOF investment is sold, or (b) December 31, 2026 under original OZ 1.0 rules
4. **10-year hold = tax-free appreciation:** If the QOF investment is held for 10+ years, **all appreciation on the QOF investment is permanently excluded from income**. The basis of the QOF investment adjusts to fair market value at sale.

### What Makes It Different from a Roth IRA

| Feature | Roth IRA | Qualified Opportunity Fund |
|---|---|---|
| **Annual contribution limit** | $7,000 ($8,000 if 50+) | **No limit** — invest the full gain amount |
| **Income limit** | Phase-out at $161K single / $240K married | **No income limit** |
| **Source of funds** | Earned income only | **Capital gains only** (any type) |
| **Tax-free growth** | Yes, after 5 years + age 59½ | **Yes, after 10-year hold** (no age requirement) |
| **Withdrawal restrictions** | Penalties before 59½ | **No age restriction** — sell after 10 years at any age |
| **Investment options** | Stocks, bonds, mutual funds | Real estate, operating businesses in designated Opportunity Zones |
| **Estate planning** | Beneficiaries inherit tax-free | Heirs get stepped-up basis on appreciation; deferred gain may still be owed |

### The Power of Scale

A client who sells a business for $10M in capital gains can deploy the **entire $10M** into a QOF. After 10 years, if that investment doubles to $20M, the $10M in appreciation is **completely tax-free**. At a 23.8% capital gains rate (20% + 3.8% NIIT), that's **$2.38M in permanent tax savings** — from a single transaction. A Roth IRA would take 1,428 years of maximum contributions to deploy the same amount.

## OZ 2.0 — The One Big Beautiful Bill Act (Signed July 4, 2025)

### What Changed

The original Opportunity Zone program was created by the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act of 2017 with a built-in sunset. OZ 2.0, passed as part of the "One Big Beautiful Bill Act," made the following changes:

| Change | OZ 1.0 (2017 TCJA) | OZ 2.0 (2025) |
|---|---|---|
| **Program duration** | Temporary (deferral ended 12/31/2026) | **Permanently extended** |
| **Zone designations** | Based on 2010 Census tracts | **New zones designated 1/1/2027 – 12/31/2033** based on 2020 Census |
| **Rural requirement** | None | **33% of new zones must be rural** |
| **Deferral benefit** | 10% step-up at 5 years, 15% at 7 years (expired 12/31/2026) | **5-year deferral with 10% step-up; 30% step-up for rural zones** |
| **Reporting** | Minimal | **Enhanced reporting and transparency requirements** |
| **10-year exclusion** | Yes — all appreciation excluded | **Maintained and permanently codified** |

### Critical 2026 Transition Rules

- **All OZ 1.0 deferred gains must be recognized on the 2026 tax return** (the original deferral deadline)
- Investors who deferred gains under OZ 1.0 will owe tax on those deferred gains regardless of whether they still hold the QOF investment
- **However**, if they continue holding the QOF investment past 10 years, the appreciation exclusion still applies
- New investments made in OZ 2.0 zones (designated after 1/1/2027) get the refreshed deferral benefits

### Bridge Strategy for 2026 Investors

For clients realizing gains in 2026 who want OZ 2.0 benefits:

1. **Deploy gains into existing OZ 1.0 QOFs now** — the 10-year appreciation exclusion still applies
2. **Wait for new OZ 2.0 zone designations** (expected 2027) — may offer better investment opportunities with rural step-up bonus
3. **Stack strategy:** Use OZ 1.0 for current gains, OZ 2.0 for future gains — build a portfolio of QOF investments with staggered 10-year clocks

## Investment Considerations

### What QOFs Can Invest In

QOFs must invest in **Qualified Opportunity Zone Property**, which includes:

- **Real estate development or substantial improvement** in designated zones
- **Operating businesses** where substantially all tangible property is in a zone
- **Qualified Opportunity Zone Stock or Partnership Interests** in businesses operating in zones

### What QOFs Cannot Invest In

- Public equities
- Private equity (unless the portfolio company operates in a zone)
- Crypto or digital assets
- Commodities
- "Sin businesses" — golf courses, country clubs, massage parlors, hot tub facilities, suntan facilities, racetracks, gambling facilities, liquor stores

### Due Diligence Red Flags

1. **Concentration risk:** Most QOF investments are in a single property or small portfolio
2. **Illiquidity:** 10-year lockup is real — no secondary market for most QOF interests
3. **Developer risk:** QOF performance depends heavily on the operator/developer
4. **Zone quality:** Not all Opportunity Zones are equal — some were genuinely distressed, others were gerrymandered in
5. **Fee stacking:** Watch for fund-level fees + property-level fees + promote structures that erode returns
6. **Compliance risk:** Failing the 90% asset test or substantial improvement test can disqualify the fund

## Risks to This Thesis

1. **Legislative risk:** Although OZ 2.0 permanently extended the program, future legislation could modify or restrict benefits
2. **Real estate market risk:** Most QOF investments are real estate — a downturn in CRE could impair returns regardless of tax benefits
3. **Inflation erosion:** 10-year lockup means purchasing power risk if inflation persists above 3%
4. **Opportunity cost:** Capital locked in a QOF can't be redeployed for 10 years — if public markets outperform, the tax savings may not compensate
5. **Complexity risk:** QOF compliance is genuinely complex — failing the 90% asset test is a disqualifying event
