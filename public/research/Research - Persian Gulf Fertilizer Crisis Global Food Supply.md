
# Persian Gulf Fertilizer Crisis — Global Food Supply Implications

## Thesis

The closure of the Strait of Hormuz and active military strikes on Persian Gulf energy and chemical infrastructure have taken approximately 30-35% of global ammonia production and 11% of global urea production offline. This represents the most significant disruption to global fertilizer supply chains since World War II. While the United States is structurally insulated — as a net energy exporter with abundant natural gas and domestic nitrogen production capacity — the rest of the world faces a chronic nitrogen fertilizer deficit that will persist for years, possibly decades.

The Social Arbitrage angle: markets are focused on oil price spikes and energy security, but the second-order effect on global food production is far more consequential and significantly underpriced. Nitrogen fertilizer is responsible for roughly 50% of global crop yields. A sustained 30%+ reduction in supply doesn't just raise food prices — it structurally reduces global caloric output. The investment implications span fertilizer producers, agricultural equipment, food commodities, shipping/logistics, and energy infrastructure.

## Background: The Persian Gulf Fertilizer Crisis

The Persian Gulf has long been the world's lowest-cost fertilizer production hub. Qatar, Saudi Arabia, Oman, and the UAE leveraged virtually free feedstock natural gas (a byproduct of oil and condensate production) to build massive ammonia and urea complexes. Qatar alone — through Qatar Fertiliser Company (QAFCO) and other facilities — produced approximately 6 million metric tons of urea annually, representing ~11% of global output.

Key facts:
- **Global urea production:** ~180 million metric tons/year
- **Persian Gulf share of global ammonia:** 30-35% (~55-60 million MT of the ~170 million MT global total)
- **Qatar's South Pars/North Dome field:** World's largest natural gas field (shared with Iran), providing near-zero-cost feedstock
- **Qatar LNG:** ~80 million MT/year capacity, ~10% of global LNG trade — facility has been struck
- **20% of global oil supply** transits the Strait of Hormuz — now offline

The QAFCO facility and related infrastructure have been hit by missile/drone attacks. Even if hostilities ended immediately, restart timelines are estimated at 6+ months for LNG, potentially longer for chemical plants that require careful recommissioning.

## Three Fertilizer Types — Impact Assessment

### 1. Potash (Potassium-based) — UNAFFECTED

| Producer | Share of Global Supply |
|---|---|
| Canada (Saskatchewan) | ~30% |
| Belarus | ~16% |
| Russia | ~16% |
| China | ~10% |
| Other | ~28% |

**Impact:** Minimal. Potash is mined, not derived from hydrocarbons. Major production centers are far from the Persian Gulf. Canadian production (Nutrien, Mosaic) is secure. Belarus/Russia supply has been constrained since 2022 sanctions but has partially rerouted.

**Risk factor:** If conflict escalates to involve Russia or disrupts Black Sea shipping, potash could tighten. But this is not the current scenario.

### 2. Phosphate — MINOR IMPACT

| Producer | Share of Global Supply |
|---|---|
| China | ~40% (but restricts exports) |
| Morocco (OCP Group) | ~18% |
| United States (Florida/Idaho) | ~10% |
| Russia | ~7% |
| Saudi Arabia (Ma'aden) | ~5% |
| Peru | ~3% |

**Impact:** Saudi Arabia's Ma'aden phosphate operations (~5% of global supply) could be disrupted, but product can be trucked west to Red Sea ports rather than shipped through the Gulf. Morocco's OCP Group — the world's largest phosphate exporter — is unaffected. The Mosaic Company (MOS) operates significant US phosphate capacity.

**Risk factor:** China has periodically restricted phosphate exports to protect domestic food security. If the crisis triggers Chinese export bans, global phosphate availability tightens materially.

### 3. Nitrogen (Ammonia/Urea) — CRITICAL

| Producer Region | Share of Global Ammonia |
|---|---|
| China | ~28% (mostly domestic use) |
| **Persian Gulf** | **~30-35%** |
| Russia | ~10% |
| United States | ~8% |
| India | ~7% |
| Indonesia | ~4% |
| Other | ~8-13% |

**Impact:** Catastrophic. The Persian Gulf's 30-35% of global ammonia production is effectively offline. This includes:
- **Qatar (QAFCO):** 6 MT urea, 3.8 MT ammonia — confirmed facility damage
- **Saudi Arabia (SABIC/Ma'aden):** ~7 MT ammonia capacity — export routes compromised
- **Oman (OMIFCO/Salalah):** ~2 MT urea — port access uncertain
- **UAE/Kuwait:** Smaller producers, under active missile attack

Nitrogen fertilizer is the most critical of the three — it's responsible for an estimated 50% of global crop yields (per Vaclav Smil's research). Without nitrogen, global food production drops by roughly half within 1-2 growing seasons.

**Why nitrogen is different:** Unlike potash (mined) and phosphate (mined), nitrogen fertilizer is synthesized from natural gas or naphtha via the Haber-Bosch process. It requires continuous energy-intensive production. You can't stockpile years of supply — global inventories typically cover 30-60 days.

## Global Food Security Impact

### Most Vulnerable (Ranked)

**1. China** — EXTREME RISK
- Imports ~20% of fertilizer inputs directly, plus feedstock energy
- Domestic nitrogen production relies on coal gasification (higher cost than gas-based)
- Limited ability to surge production without massive energy cost increase
- 1.4 billion people, food security is existential political risk
- Likely to impose export bans on domestic fertilizer, worsening global shortage

**2. South/Southeast Asia (India, Bangladesh, Vietnam, Thailand)** — HIGH RISK
- India imports ~30% of its fertilizer (primarily urea from Persian Gulf)
- India has strategic interest in securing direct Persian Gulf access — could reshape its security posture
- Bangladesh, Vietnam, Thailand — major rice producers dependent on imported nitrogen
- Subsistence farming in these regions means price spikes → humanitarian crisis

**3. Sub-Saharan Africa** — HIGH RISK
- Already uses far less fertilizer per hectare than global average
- Price spikes make fertilizer unaffordable for smallholder farmers
- Food production drops → famine risk in East/West Africa
- Limited domestic production capacity

**4. Europe** — MODERATE-HIGH RISK
- Already saw fertilizer crisis in 2022 after Russian gas disruption
- Some European nitrogen plants mothballed (BASF, Yara)
- Can source from US but at higher cost + shipping
- Agricultural output at risk if fertilizer prices triple

**5. Brazil** — MODERATE RISK
- World's largest fertilizer importer
- Major soybean/corn producer for global export
- Has been diversifying sources but still dependent on imports
- Domestic production covers only ~15-20% of needs

### Least Vulnerable

**United States** — STRUCTURALLY INSULATED
- Net energy exporter with abundant natural gas ($2-3/MMBtu vs $10-15+ internationally)
- Domestic nitrogen capacity (~15 MT ammonia) covers most needs
- Can surge production — CF Industries, Nutrien, LSB have expansion optionality
- Will export fertilizer at premium prices to allies
- Food production secure; agricultural exports become strategic asset

## Energy Market Spillover

The fertilizer crisis doesn't exist in isolation — it's layered on top of a 20% reduction in global oil supply:

- **Crude oil:** Strait of Hormuz closure removes ~17-20 million barrels/day. Prices likely sustained above $120-150/bbl.
- **Natural gas:** Qatar LNG (10% of global supply) offline. European and Asian spot LNG prices spike. US Henry Hub benefits as export demand surges.
- **Naphtha:** Key alternative feedstock for nitrogen production. With crude spiking, naphtha costs rise, making non-US nitrogen production even more expensive.
- **Shipping:** MATX (Matson) and global shipping benefit from trade rerouting. Longer voyages around Africa increase ton-mile demand.

## Risks

1. **Rapid ceasefire.** If hostilities end quickly and Gulf infrastructure is restored within months, the fertilizer price spike could reverse sharply. Companies that surged on supply fears would correct.

2. **Demand destruction.** If fertilizer prices triple, farmers in developing nations simply use less. This reduces demand but also reduces food production — a humanitarian crisis, not a market rebalancing.

3. **Chinese coal-to-fertilizer surge.** China could ramp coal gasification-based nitrogen production. Higher cost, higher emissions, but feasible at scale. Could partially offset Gulf losses over 12-18 months.

4. **US export controls.** If the crisis deepens, the US government could restrict fertilizer exports to protect domestic food security. This would cap upside for CF, Nutrien, etc.

5. **Valuation risk.** Fertilizer stocks tend to be cyclical. If this is priced as a structural shift and it turns out to be temporary, downside is significant.

6. **Russian supply rerouting.** Russia produces ~10% of global ammonia. If sanctions ease or Russian product finds new routes, it partially offsets Gulf losses.
